# Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP): Clinical DLP Framework

**Role:** Expert Statistician

## 1. Causal Model: Informative Missingness (MNAR)
The primary objective is to move beyond the "Missing at Random" (MAR) assumption, which fails in psychiatric digital phenotyping. We model the data generation process as a **Missing Not At Random (MNAR)** mechanism.

### 1.1 Structural Causal Model (SCM) & DAG
We define the following variables:
- $S_t$: Latent Psychiatric State (e.g., Severity of Depression/Paranoia).
- $X_t$: Observed Covariates (e.g., Battery level, Connectivity, Time of Day).
- $Y_t^*$: True Behavioral Metric (e.g., GPS mobility, Social interaction).
- $M_t$: Missingness Indicator (1 if $Y_t^*$ is missing, 0 otherwise).
- $Y_t$: Observed Behavioral Metric ($Y_t = Y_t^*$ if $M_t = 0$, else $null$).

**The Refined DAG:**
1. $S_t \rightarrow Y_t^*$: The latent state causes the true behavior.
2. $S_t \rightarrow M_t$: **(MNAR - Clinical)** High symptom severity (e.g., lethargy) directly causes cessation of use.
3. $Y_t^* \rightarrow M_t$: **(MNAR - Behavioral)** Specific behaviors (e.g., deviant mobility) trigger the user to disable tracking.
4. $X_t \rightarrow M_t$: **(MAR/MCAR)** Low battery or poor GPS signal causes technical dropout.
5. $S_{t-1} \rightarrow S_t$: Temporal dependency of the psychiatric state.

**Refinement:** The Data Scientist's initial model likely overlooked the $Y_t^* \rightarrow M_t$ path. In clinical practice, the *action* being measured often causes the missingness (e.g., the act of traveling to a "forbidden" location causes the patient to turn off the phone). We must explicitly model this to avoid biased treatment effect estimates.

## 2. Uncertainty Quantification: Bayesian Reliability
To quantify the reliability of the synthetic data generated by the framework, we will implement **Bayesian Posterior Predictive Checks (PPCs)**.

### 2.1 Bayesian Credible Intervals (BCIs)
- **Method:** We will use **Gaussian Process (GP) Priors** with a Matérn kernel for the latent state $S_t$ to allow for non-stationary trajectories.
- **Implementation:** For each synthetic patient $i$, we compute the posterior distribution $P(Y_{i, t}^* | Y_{i, obs}, X_{i, t}, \mathcal{M})$, where $\mathcal{M}$ is our generative model.
- **Metric:** The **Synthetic Reliability Score (SRS)** will be defined as the width of the 95% Bayesian Credible Interval.
    - If the BCI width $> \delta$ (predefined threshold), the synthetic data point is flagged as "High Uncertainty" and should be down-weighted during downstream model training.

## 3. Validation Strategy: Causal Transportability
We must prove that our model, trained on synthetic data corrected for MNAR, generalizes to real-world "disengaged" cohorts.

### 3.1 Transportability Protocol
1. **Source Population ($\Pi_s$):** Synthetic cohort with simulated MNAR based on Harvard/Onnela Lab parameters.
2. **Target Population ($\Pi_t$):** Unseen, real-world cohort of patients who experienced a "Clinical Dropout" (identified via retrospective analysis of the Beiwe or mindLAMP datasets).
3. **Selection Nodes ($S$):** We will identify $S$-nodes where $\Pi_s$ and $\Pi_t$ differ (e.g., baseline smartphone literacy, socio-economic status).
4. **Validation Metric:** **Transported Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic (T-AUROC)**.
    - We compare our model against a "Naive Imputation" baseline (e.g., mean imputation or MICE).
    - Success is defined as a statistically significant improvement ($p < 0.01$) in T-AUROC on $\Pi_t$, proving the model has learned the *causal mechanism* of relapse rather than just technical artifacts.

## 4. Statistical Rigor & Sample Size
- **Sample Size Estimation:** To detect an effect size of $d = 0.5$ in T-AUROC improvement with 90% power ($\alpha = 0.05$), we require a minimum of $N=250$ synthetic trajectories and $N=50$ real-world validation trajectories.
- **Metric Robustness:** We will use the **Brier Score** to evaluate probability calibration, as psychiatric risk forecasting requires accurate probability estimates for clinical intervention triggers, not just binary classification.
- **Sensitivity Analysis:** We will perform a "Tipping Point Analysis" to determine how strong the $S_t \rightarrow M_t$ relationship must be before our causal model's superiority vanishes.

---
**Statistical Limitation Block:**
*The proposed DAG assumes that all technical causes of missingness ($X_t$) are observed. If unobserved technical confounders (e.g., unlogged OS-level process kills) exist, the distinction between "Technical" and "Clinical" dropouts may be blurred, leading to potential misclassification of risk.*
