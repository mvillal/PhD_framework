# Out with AI, in with the Psychiatrist (Jacobs et al., 2023)

## Summary
Investigates "human-labeling bias," finding that clinicians are more likely to trust and follow treatment recommendations when they are attributed to a human peer rather than an AI.

## Methodology
- **Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT):** A study where the same set of treatment recommendations were randomly attributed to either a "senior psychiatrist" or an "AI system."

## Findings
- **Human-Labeling Bias:** Clinicians showed a significant preference for recommendations labeled as human-sourced, even when the recommendations themselves were identical to those labeled as AI-sourced.
- **Algorithm Aversion:** The study highlights a persistent "algorithm aversion" in clinical settings, where human expertise is inherently trusted more than machine-generated insights.

## Relevance
- **Psychiatry:** Critical for understanding the barriers to AI adoption in mental health.
- **Implementation Science:** Suggests that the *presentation* and *attribution* of AI insights are as important as the accuracy of the models themselves.

## Key Findings
- **Trust and Attribution:** Trust in a recommendation is heavily influenced by its perceived source, independent of its actual quality.
- **Overcoming Aversion:** Highlights the need for strategies to build trust in AI, potentially through better integration with human workflows and more transparent attribution.
