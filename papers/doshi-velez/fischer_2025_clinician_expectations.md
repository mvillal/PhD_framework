# AI for Mental Health: Understanding Clinician Expectations and Priorities

## 📋 Executive Summary
What do clinicians actually want from AI? This survey-based study reveals a mismatch between the AI research focus (treatment prescription) and clinician needs (trajectory monitoring). Clinicians prioritize AI that can alert them to patient decline over AI that tells them which drug to prescribe.

## 🛠️ Core Methodology
- **Structured Clinician Survey:** A comprehensive survey of **53 clinicians** (psychiatrists, psychologists, and social workers).
- **Preference Elicitation:** Using hypothetical scenarios to rank AI features (e.g., "Predicting relapse" vs. "Suggesting dosage").
- **Thematic Analysis:** Identifying recurring concerns and desired "entry points" for AI in the clinic.

## 📊 Dataset & Experimental Setup
- **Data Source:** **53 clinicians** from diverse mental health settings.
- **Sample Size:** 53 participants.
- **Features:** Job role, years of experience, current use of technology, and Likert-scale responses to AI features.
- **Evaluation Metrics:** Priority Ranking, Perceived Utility, and Qualitative Alignment.

## 💡 Key Findings
- **Trajectory Monitoring Priority:** Clinicians ranked "monitoring for relapse" and "identifying patient trajectories" as significantly more valuable than automated treatment recommendations.
- **Prescriptive Aversion:** There is significant resistance to AI that makes "high-level" decisions (like prescribing), whereas "low-level" support (like scheduling or monitoring) is welcomed.
- **The "Safety Net" Role:** Clinicians see AI's best role as a "second set of eyes" that catches what they might miss during a busy shift.

## 🩺 Clinical Relevance & Impact
Provides a roadmap for "Human-Centered AI" in psychiatry. Instead of building better "treatment suggestors," labs should focus on **trajectory monitoring** and "early warning systems" to align with the professional identity and needs of mental health providers.

## 🔬 Critical Review (Antagonic Perspective)
Clinician "expectations" are often shaped by a lack of understanding of what AI can *actually* do. By focusing only on what clinicians *say* they want, we might miss opportunities for transformative (rather than just incremental) AI improvements.
