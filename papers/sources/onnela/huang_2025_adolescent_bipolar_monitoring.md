# Design and feasibility of smartphone-based digital phenotyping for long-term mental health monitoring in adolescents

## 📋 Executive Summary
This study evaluates the feasibility of using the **Beiwe** platform for long-term (18-month) monitoring of adolescents with bipolar disorder. It demonstrates that high-frequency passive sensing and EMA are acceptable and informative for tracking clinical trajectories in this vulnerable population.

## 🛠️ Core Methodology
- **Longitudinal Monitoring:** 18-month duration focusing on transition periods (e.g., school starts, medication changes).
- **Multimodal Sensing:** Combination of GPS, accelerometer, call/text logs, and weekly EMA surveys.
- **Adherence Analysis:** Quantifying data density and participant engagement over long periods.

## 📊 Dataset & Experimental Setup
- **Data Source:** High-risk adolescents with Bipolar I and II disorders.
- **Metrics:** Data completeness, correlation between passive features (social activity) and clinical mood scores.

## 💡 Key Findings
- **High Feasibility:** Adolescents show sustained engagement with passive sensing over 18 months.
- **Early Warning Signs:** Changes in social communication and mobility patterns often precede formal mood relapses by 1-2 weeks.

## 🩺 Clinical Relevance & Impact
Enables proactive intervention for adolescents with bipolar disorder, a group at high risk for suicide and severe functional impairment. Provides a model for "continuous" clinical care.

## 🔬 Critical Review (Antagonic Perspective)
The long-term nature of the study risks "monitoring fatigue." There are also significant ethical concerns regarding the intensive surveillance of adolescents, which might impact their social development and sense of privacy.

## 🔗 Discovery & Next Steps
- **Ancestor Discovery:** Torous et al. (2016) on the early feasibility of smartphone monitoring in psychiatry.
- **Descendant Discovery:** Integrating these long-term trajectories into automated suicide forecasting models (e.g., Nock et al., 2026).
