# Out with AI, in with the psychiatrist: a preference for human-derived clinical decision support in depression care

## 📋 Executive Summary
This paper identifies "Human-Labeling Bias"—a form of algorithm aversion where clinicians inherently trust recommendations more when they believe the source is a human peer rather than an AI. This bias persists even when the underlying content is identical, highlighting a critical psychological barrier to the clinical implementation of psychiatric AI.

## 🛠️ Core Methodology
- **Source Attribution Study:** A randomized design where identical clinical recommendations were attributed to either "AI" or a "Psychiatrist."
- **Survey Analysis:** Evaluation of how source attribution influences clinician ratings of recommendation quality and their willingness to implement the advice.
- **Quantitative Preference Scoring:** Statistical analysis comparing trust scores and implementation likelihood between the two attribution groups.

## 📊 Dataset & Experimental Setup
- **Data Source:** Survey of practicing clinicians evaluating Clinical Support Tools (CSTs) like patient summaries and medication suggestions.
- **Participants:** n = 100+ clinicians with varying levels of experience in depression care.
- **Evaluation Metrics:** Likert-scale ratings for "Reliability," "Utility," and "Trust."

## 💡 Key Findings
- **Significant Human Preference:** Clinicians rated identical medication recommendations significantly more favorably when they were attributed to a human psychiatrist.
- **Bias in Correctness:** Even correct recommendations were less likely to be adopted if the clinician believed they originated from an AI system.
- **Algorithm Aversion:** The findings confirm "algorithm aversion" in high-stakes psychiatric decision-making, where clinicians are more forgiving of human error than AI error.

## 🩺 Clinical Relevance & Impact
The "human-AI chasm" suggests that high-performing psychiatric algorithms will still face significant barriers to adoption unless they are presented as part of a "human-in-the-loop" system that emphasizes clinical collaboration rather than replacement.

## 🔬 Critical Review (Antagonic Perspective)
The study relies on self-reported intent rather than actual clinical behavior. In real-world settings, "Human-Labeling Bias" might be mitigated by hospital-wide mandates or the proven, long-term performance of the AI tool.
